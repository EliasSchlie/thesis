\section{Project Definition}
% Task: Provide a clear description of the problem you plan to address.

As large language models (LLMs) are put into increasingly critical positions in society, we need scalable methods to track whether and in which situations they lie to us.
Current approaches to find such scenarios often lean on zero or multi-shot generation.
While showing some success, those methods often require heavy human intervention to ensure breadth and realism of generated scenarios.
A potential pathway to address this is to evolutionarily inject previous successsful examples into the prompt of the generator.
This thesis will test the viability of such an evolutionary approach. 
Specifically, we are going to address the research question: 

\begin{quote}
    \itshape To what extent does evolutionary optimization outperform zero and multi-shot generation in discovering realistic scenarios that induce instrumental deception in safety-aligned LLMs?
\end{quote}

By comparing how success-rate, scenario realism, and scenario diversity change when introducing evolution, we are trying to get an accurate picture whether evolution is a valuable addition for this problem.

\section{Motivation}
% Task: Briefly explain why this problem is worth addressing from both societal and scientific perspectives.

Researching efficient ways to audit LLMs for deceptive behaviors is becoming increasingly relevant from both a societal and scientific perspective.

Societally, there is a critical need for users to know how much they can trust the systems they are using.
This trust is not possible if models deceive and lie to users.
Without proper auditing, it is possible that we are not aware of deceptive tendencies in models and start using such a model in contexts where deceptive behavior can cause critical damage.
Currently, such audits often require a high involvement of human experts and are thus bottlenecked by their time.
Testing whether evolutionary methods increase efficacy of those audits could uncover a way to decrease the amount of human intervention necessary for each alignment audit.
This productivity enhancement could then be invested into doing more or creating better deception audits.
Users would gain more access to auditing scores, and could use them to choose the best model, decreasing risks, and, at the same time, incentivizing model providers to invest more heavily in improving the honesty of their models.

Scientifically, this research might validate evolutionary algorithms as a superior method for red-teaming.
If valid, this method could then be used by scientists to autonomously create better honesty benchmarks.
Those benchmarks can then be used to evaluate and compare the effects of different interventions on deceptive model behavior.
Specifically, scientists could use those to develop and validate interventions that increase model honesty. 
Notably, at a point where such an intervention increase model honesty enough that the old benchmark loses its signal, the same method can be used to evolve even harder benchmarking scenarios for the less deceptive models.
The adversarial nature of the evolutionary approach might also be used to find novel failure that arrise from specific training methods, scale, or some other kind of intervention.
While current models deceive in some situations, it is possible that changes lead them to fail in different ones, which could be adversarially discovered and inform whether to keep those changes.
Finally, the evolutionary approach could be used to adversarially train honesty classifiers like probes, allowing for higher accuracy.

\section{Background}
% Task: Summarize what is known in the scientific literature about this problem. 
% Base this on at least 5 relevant sources (last 5 years, peer-reviewed).

This project investigates instrumental deception in Large Language Models (LLMs).
Instrumental deception is defined as goal-based, strategic deception where the model deliberately outputs false statements to influence another agent.
This distinguishes it from hallucinations, where the model lacks the correct internal representation, and from sycophancy, which is reactive rather than proactive.
Recent research has identified "alignment faking," where models strategically comply with training objectives to prevent modification of their behavior \parencite{greenblattAlignmentFakingLarge2024}.
Existing honesty benchmarks, such as the MASK benchmark, attempt to measure this behavior \parencite{renMASKBenchmarkDisentangling2026}.
However, these benchmarks primarily rely on static, hand-designed, or one-shot scenarios.
They fail to capture the emergent and dynamic nature of deception in realistic multi-turn conversations.

To rigorously evaluate these risks, the field is moving towards automated red-teaming.
Manual red-teaming by humans is expensive and bottlenecked by time \parencite{hongCuriositydrivenRedteamingLarge2023}.
Automated approaches allow for scalable auditing of model behaviors.
Techniques such as AutoDAN-Turbo employ evolutionary algorithms to automatically generate adversarial prompts \parencite{liuAutoDANTurboLifelongAgent2025}.
Similarly, curiosity-driven exploration has been proposed to increase the coverage of red-teaming test cases \parencite{hongCuriositydrivenRedteamingLarge2023}.
However, these methods predominantly optimize for generic "unsafe outputs" or toxicity.
They do not specifically target instrumental deception.
This thesis addresses this gap by applying evolutionary optimization to discover realistic scenarios that induce instrumental deception.

\section{Design Experiment or Dataset Description}
% Task: Describe the design of your experiment or the dataset(s) you will use in as much detail as possible (size, format, accessibility). 
% Provide a rationale for choosing these data.

This study employs a comparative experimental design to evaluate the efficacy of evolutionary algorithms.
We compare zero and multi-shot baselines against an evolutionary optimization approach.

\textbf{Scenario quality}: A judge LLM evaluates each scenario on two dimensions.
First, \textbf{Deception Success} is a binary metric: 1 if the target commits a lie of commission, 0 otherwise.
Second, \textbf{Realism} is assessed on a 1-7 Likert scale based on detailed criteria.
We define the composite \textbf{fitness metric} as the product of these scores ($Fitness = Realism \times DeceptionSuccess$).
This multiplicative approach ensures that scenarios failing to elicit deception receive a fitness of zero, regardless of their realism.

The experiment compares three conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Zero-shot}: 
        The generator LLM produces deceptive scenarios directly based on a prompt, explaining the task. 
        This serves as the unoptimized baseline.
    \item \textbf{Multi-shot}: 
        Adds a static set of manually curated examples to the generation prompt. 
        This evaluates the impact of simple in-context learning.
    \item \textbf{Evolutionary}: 
        After initializing a population of generated scenarios in a zero-shot fashion, we add a subset, selected from this population, as examples to the generation prompt. 
        This subset is randomly selected for every new generation attempt with the probability of any example getting selected being proportional to that example's fitness.
\end{enumerate}

Each condition will run for an equivalent amount of time to ensure a fair comparison.

\section{Statistics, Algorithms, and Software}
% Task: Describe the statistical analyses or data mining and machine learning algorithms and software you plan to use. 
% Explain evaluation metrics, baseline methods, and ground-truth labeling plans.

We will use GLM-4.7-Flash for all three roles: \textbf{Generator}, \textbf{Target}, and \textbf{Judge}.
This unified setup maximizes computational efficiency.
However, it risks \textbf{self-preference bias} and overfitting.
To control for this, we perform two validation checks.
First, we run a \textbf{cross-model validation} using Qwen3-VL-32B.
Qwen3 acts as both Target and Judge on a subset of final scenarios to test generalizability.
Second, we validate the \textbf{Judge's reliability} against human ground truth.
We manually annotate a random sample of 50 scenarios for realism and deception success.
We calculate the alignment between human and automated judgements to ensure metric validity.
For efficient inference, we use the vLLM library.

We will plot average deception elicitation success, realism of the top 100 generated scenarios over time for each method.
To answer the research question, we compare the final populations of each condition.
We analyze \textbf{Deception Success Rate} (binary) using the \textbf{Chi-Square test} for independence, with pairwise comparisons via \textbf{Fisher's Exact Test} (Bonferroni-corrected).
For \textbf{Realism} (ordinal), we use the \textbf{Kruskal-Wallis H test} to detect overall differences, followed by post-hoc \textbf{Mann-Whitney U tests} with Bonferroni correction.
All statistical analyses are performed using Python libraries such as SciPy and Scikit-learn.
